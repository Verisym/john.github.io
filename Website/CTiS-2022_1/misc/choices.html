How Choices affect Data Privacy in Social Media

The choices that revolve around keeping your data safe online centre around the degree to which you choose to engage in social media or not. However, if the individual chooses to engage in social media, there are preventative measures they could take in protecting their privacy. This could be by changing the default privacy settings on the social media app or website to limit the number of information it shares. Or restricting the information you personally post online. In general, you want to avoid sharing sensitive information, like your nearest school, current workplace, or political positions or sensitive financial data to avoid cyber criminals stealing your identity online (D. 2021).

Social media has created an environment where users are endlessly encouraged by all the various social media platforms and all the different websites and apps that run through them to give up their personal data for the sake of ‘convenience’ through targeted advertising. This approach has led to millions of user's personal data being stored with only a handful of companies, the lack of competition in this sector ultimately leading to user data being at risk as all of it is stored in the same place ‘Should social media data be misused, the risks to individual users' privacy and well-being can be substantial’ (Minin et al., 2021).

To combat this, users have a few things up their sleeve they can do. The first, and most obvious, being simply limiting how much personal information they put online. Choosing not to share their current or previous locations with social media apps, not allowing these apps access to their private data on their phones, not uploading pictures that may contain themselves or any information leading to their identity. However, after taking these precautions, you may be left without much reason to engage in social media at all, as their main purpose is to connect with other people. It creates a great catch-22, if you engage too much on social media, you lose your anonymity. If you engage to little, you miss out on quite a large chunk of socialising and keeping up with your friends.

Most social media applications ask for use of cookies, user enabled tracking of their online footprint, in order to create a profile and allow for more personalised recommendations. Multinational conglomerates linking and tracking these same users over all social media platforms is what seems to be where many issues lie. Creating user profiles through data mining and then selling the data they have mined for profit to advertising companies. ‘Personalized recommendation is crucial to help users find pertinent information. It often relies on a large collection of user data, in particular users' online activity (e.g., tagging/rating/checking-in) on social media, to mine user preference. However, releasing such user activity data makes users vulnerable to inference attacks, as private data (e.g., gender) can often be inferred from the users' activity data’ (Yang, 2019).

The tools people have at their disposal to protect themselves online range in complexity and effectiveness and have also expanded over the years. From simply not ticking a box that allows social media websites collect their cookies, to buying expensive anti-virus software that stops all passage to any website that it deems dangerous or is trying to take information from behind the scenes, or a VPN (Virtual Private network) to simply add an extra level of encryption between users' private data and everyone else.

To conclude, there are countless choices that have been created in order to keep your data private on social media. From utilising ever advancing digital tools to keep your private information hidden when engaging in social media, to simply choosing to be mindful of what you post, every action and decision made whilst engaging with social media impacts how safe your data is. There is plenty that is in the control of the user, but not being mindful can quickly give that data and power over to someone else.
